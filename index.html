Why Advisor-Led Content

What this program is, what it asks of you, and what it's designed to do.

Short-form video featuring EP Wealth advisors speaking directly to camera tends to build trust and generate engagement in ways that other creative often doesn't. The reason is straightforward: people trust people. When a qualified advisor addresses a financial concern clearly and specifically, it carries a credibility that a generic or stock image cannot replicate.

This guide is designed to make the process as straightforward as possible. You don't need to be a natural on camera. You don't need production experience. You need a phone, decent light, and something real to say. The rest of this guide handles everything else.

What this asks of you: Roughly 60 minutes every two to three weeks — a filming session that produces six to eight short videos. The marketing team handles editing, captioning, distribution, and paid amplification. Your job is to show up and speak to what you know.

WHAT THIS IS

Authentic, direct, advisor-led content

Short videos of EP Wealth advisors speaking directly to specific financial concerns. No studio. No script. The advisor's expertise and presence is the asset.

WHAT THIS IS NOT

Polished brand advertising

This is not a TV commercial or a scripted corporate video. The moment it looks produced and performed, it risks losing the quality that makes it effective.

WHAT YOU GET

Personal brand and attributed leads

Leads generated through your content are attributed to the content you produce. Your LinkedIn has the potential to grow. EP Wealth covers production support and media spend.

01

Setup & Gear

Everything needed before pressing record. Achievable for under $500 total. No production crew required.

CAMERA

iPhone 14+ or any recent flagship

Use the back camera, not the front. The rear camera on any recent iPhone or Android produces significantly better image quality. Film vertically (portrait) for all social formats. If you have an iPhone 14 or newer, that is all the camera you need. Do not purchase a separate camera.

MICROPHONE — MOST IMPORTANT PURCHASE

Rode Wireless GO II or DJI Mic 2

Audio quality matters just as much as video quality. Poor audio causes people to stop watching regardless of what is being said. The Rode Wireless GO II (~$299) or DJI Mic 2 (~$199) clip on and connect wirelessly. Either one would greatly enhance the quality of the videos.

STABILIZATION

Tripod — ideal

The camera should be completely still. An adjustable tripod ($30–60) works for all standard filming setups. The DJI OM 6 gimbal (~$159) is an option if you want to film while moving. For most sessions, a tripod is all you need. Handheld is not ideal but okay in a pinch.

LIGHTING

Natural light first, key light second

A window with indirect natural light, camera facing the window and you facing the camera, is the best and lowest-cost lighting setup available. For indoor or low-light situations, an Elgato Key Light (~$199) or a basic ring light ($40–80) works well. Avoid overhead fluorescent office lighting on its own.

TOTAL INVESTMENT

Minimum (phone + tripod + ring light): ~$100. Recommended: Phone + Rode Wireless GO II + Elgato Key Light + tripod ≈ $500.

02

Environment & Lighting

Where and how the shot is framed. The background sends a signal before you speak a word.

OPTION A — PREFERRED

Office with natural depth

Your actual office with bookshelves or a window visible in the background, slightly out of focus. Real environment signals authenticity. Clean but not staged. This is the strongest default setting.

OPTION B — STRONG

Plain wall, neutral tone

A clean cream, white, or dark wall with you in sharp focus and nothing distracting behind. Works well for content where a text overlay will appear on screen. Clean, professional, widely usable.

OPTION C — CONTEXTUAL

Meeting room or off-site location

A client meeting space or a clean coffee shop environment. Adds authenticity for more conversational content. Ensure background noise is manageable with the wireless mic.

LIGHTING: DO

Face a window — natural light front-on is the best available light

Ensure your eyes are bright — light should create a visible catchlight in the eyes

Film in morning or late afternoon for warmer natural light

Position a key light at roughly 45° to your face, slightly above eye level

Aim for slightly warm color temperature — it reads as more natural on camera

LIGHTING: DON'T

Film with a window behind you — creates a silhouette

Rely only on overhead office fluorescents — creates unflattering shadows

Use a ring light centered directly in front — creates a flat look that can undermine authority

Mix warm and cool light sources — looks inconsistent on screen

THE 60-SECOND LIGHT CHECK

Before filming, look at yourself on the phone screen. Are your eyes clearly visible and lit? Is there any strong shadow across one side of your face? Is the background distractingly busy or dark? If eyes are bright and your face is evenly lit, the lighting is good.

03

Filming Technique

Frame, distance, eye contact, and format.

FRAMING

Medium close-up. Leave room in the top third for negative space.

Your frame should show head and chest — not full body, not extreme close-up. Leave the upper third of the frame for negative space, this will be used for text or captions. This is the composition that tends to read as authoritative and engaged. See the framing guide in Section 07 for a visual reference.

DISTANCE

2–3 feet from camera.

The camera should be 2–3 feet from your face. This creates conversational intimacy. Anything further reads as a stage presentation. Move the tripod closer rather than zooming in — digital zoom degrades quality. Physical distance is the only variable worth adjusting here.

EYE CONTACT

Look at the lens, not yourself.

Look directly at the camera lens — not at your own face on the screen. Looking at the screen instead of the lens reads as avoiding eye contact, which can undermine the trust and directness these videos are built around. If it helps, place a small sticker or piece of tape next to the lens as a focus point.

FORMAT SETTINGS

4K 30fps. Film vertically.

Set your iPhone to 4K 30fps in Camera settings before filming. Film everything vertically (portrait orientation). This gives the most flexibility across platforms — vertical content can be reframed for horizontal if needed. Horizontal content cannot be made vertical without significant quality loss.

THE HOOK SHOT — START HERE

One idea. Under a minute. Ideally around 15s. Stop the scroll.

Structure:

Seconds 0–3: The hook. No preamble.

Seconds 3–30: One supporting insight or scenario.

Seconds 30–45: The resolution and CTA — what should the viewer take from this?


Example: "Your RSUs vested last month. The tax hit is already locked in. Did anyone call you before that happened?" [30 seconds explaining why the planning window is before the vest, not after]


This is the primary format for building awareness and testing which topics resonate.

THE SCENARIO — STRONGEST FOR TRUST

Anonymous client situation as proof of insight.

Structure:

"I worked with someone recently who…" → describe the situation

The gap or decision they were about to make

What changed when they had the right plan


Critical rule: Always fully anonymous — no names, no identifying details. The situation is what resonates. The viewer maps their own circumstances onto it.


Compliance note: Frame as illustrative, not as a testimonial. "Here's a situation I've seen" rather than "here's what we achieved for a client."

THE ASSESSMENT BRIDGE — WARM AUDIENCES ONLY

A direct invitation to get your numbers run.

This format runs to retargeting audiences only — not cold traffic.


Bridge the insight from earlier content to a specific invitation: "We run an actual analysis of your specific situation — income, assets, tax exposure, timing — and show you what the numbers actually look like. No pitch. No products. Just your numbers."


This format should generally not be the first thing someone sees from EP Wealth.

THE TRUTH DROP — HIGHEST SHAREABILITY

A surprising fact, explained plainly.

Structure:

Open with the surprising stat or fact.

Explain why it's true and why most people don't know it.

Close with the single most important implication.


Example: "Up to 85% of your Social Security benefit can be subject to federal income tax. Most people find this out at tax time — not before. The window to plan around it is before you start collecting, not after."


Works across all formats including as a standalone static image with kinetic text.

05

Delivery & On-Camera Presence

How to speak, move, and carry yourself. This is the hardest thing to describe and the most important to get right.

The most common issue advisors face on camera is performing — shifting into a slightly more formal, more careful version of themselves when they feel the camera. Viewers pick up on this quickly and it tends to register as either uncomfortable or inauthentic. The goal is the opposite: be less polished, not more. Speak the way you would to a client you've known for three years.

1

Start mid-thought — no intro

Never open with "Hi, I'm [name] from EP Wealth Advisors." The camera is already rolling when you start speaking. The hook is the first thing to say. Your name and firm appear in the caption and the end card — they don't need to open the video. Starting mid-thought signals that you have something worth saying immediately.

2

Pause before the key words

A brief pause before the most important word or phrase in a sentence creates emphasis and gives the viewer time to absorb what's coming. "Most people don't realize their inherited IRA could cost their beneficiaries… forty cents on the dollar." The pause builds anticipation and prevents the rushed cadence that makes delivery sound scripted. It also slows the pace in a way that works well on camera.

3

Speak to one person, not an audience

Imagine you're telling one specific person — a current client you know well — something they need to hear over coffee. Not presenting to a room, not recording for posterity. The conversational intimacy of speaking to one person is what tends to make these videos feel real and direct. If it sounds like a keynote, that's a signal to reset.

4

Let the natural imperfections stay

Small pauses, a natural sentence restart, an "uh" — these often register as features, not flaws. They signal that this is genuine and unrehearsed, which is part of what makes it worth watching. Reshoot if you lose the thread entirely, if external noise interrupts, or if the delivery is so stilted it reads as uncomfortable. Otherwise, the first take that feels real is usually the one to keep. Many times, the best thing to do is keep talking, even if you stumble. We can edit those imperfections out many times. Especially if you record the same script several times. 

5

Physical presence: engaged but still

Minimal swaying, pacing, or fidgeting — the camera amplifies movement significantly. Natural hand gestures while speaking are fine and can add energy. Lean slightly forward toward the camera — it reads as engaged and confident. Leaning back reads as detached. Keep your head level; chin slightly down rather than up.

6

Warm up before filming

Do not film cold. Have a brief conversation about anything before the camera rolls — the goal is to get into natural conversational mode before you start. Alternatively, film a 60-second throwaway take on something off-topic just to break any camera anxiety. The first real take after a warm-up is almost always better than the first cold take.

DELIVERY: DO

Start with the hook immediately — no preamble

Use natural pauses before key words

Speak slightly slower than your normal pace

Use specific numbers and scenarios — specificity signals credibility

Cover one idea per video

End after the point lands

DELIVERY: DON'T

Introduce yourself at the start

Read from a script — use talking points at most

Try to cover multiple ideas in one video

Film more than 90 seconds for a single concept

Do more than 3 takes per hook — the first real take is usually best

The 9:16 Framing Guide

How to position yourself within the vertical frame so that text overlays, captions, and your face all have the right space.

All content is filmed in 9:16 (portrait) format. This is the native format for Meta Reels, Instagram Stories, LinkedIn video, and YouTube Shorts. The frame is divided into three horizontal zones using the rule of thirds. Understanding how these zones work determines where you position yourself and where text hooks and captions will be placed in post.

TOP THIRD — TEXT HOOK ZONE

Leave the top third as negative space. This is where the marketing team places the text hook overlay — the large, bold opening line that stops the scroll for viewers watching on mute. If you crowd this area with your face or body, the text overlay becomes unreadable. Position yourself so this zone stays clear. You can navigate to your phone settings, find camera settings, then 

MIDDLE THIRD — YOUR EYES

Your eyes should land in the middle third — ideally near the upper intersection of the rule of thirds grid. This is the compositional sweet spot that reads as authoritative and engaged without appearing to crowd the frame. Adjust your tripod height until this is achieved before filming.

BOTTOM THIRD — CHEST, SHOULDERS, CAPTIONS

Your chest and shoulders occupy the lower third. The marketing team places closed captions here. Avoid busy patterns or bright graphics on clothing in this area — they compete visually with caption text. Solid colors or subtle textures work best.

QUICK SETUP CHECK

Before filming, hold your phone up and look at the live view. Are your eyes sitting in the middle third of the frame? Is there clear space above your head in the top third? If yes, the framing is correct. If your eyes are in the center of the frame, raise the tripod or step back slightly.

07

The Filming Session

How to run a session efficiently. The goal is 6–8 usable videos in 90 minutes.

SESSION CADENCE

Once every 2–3 weeks. Each 60-minute session produces 6–8 videos that are edited, captioned, and released across 2–3 weeks. This cadence maintains content freshness without requiring significant ongoing time.

1

Pre-Session Brief (sent 48 hours before)

The marketing team sends a brief with 8–10 hook options 48 hours before each session. You select 6 that genuinely resonate with you — the constraint matters. Hooks you find hollow or inauthentic will show on camera. You can also bring 1–2 of your own based on recent client conversations. Those often produce the strongest content. We will just need to get these approved by Compliance before using them in market.

2

Day-Of Setup (15 minutes)

Set up the tripod at correct height (lens at your eye level). Run the light check — 60 seconds maximum. Attach the wireless mic. Film one 30-second warm-up take on anything at all — this is discarded, it just breaks the camera anxiety. Clean the camera lens with a cloth. These 15 minutes prevent an hour of reshoots later.

3

Film Hook Shots First

Film the 2–3 most direct or challenging hooks first, while your energy is highest. Try avoiding reading directly off the script if possible. Give each hook a maximum of 3 takes. The first take that feels real is usually the right one. If a hook isn't working after 3 takes, move on and return to it later. Forcing it produces exactly the kind of stiff delivery you're trying to avoid.

4

Reset Between Takes

Take 30–60 seconds between hooks to break the state — a brief conversation, a drink of water, a physical shake-out. The camera accumulates tension over time. Regular resets prevent the cumulative stiffening that makes the last few videos of a session noticeably worse than the first.

5

End with Conversational Content

Close the session with Scenario or Truth Drop formats — more conversational, lower pressure than direct hook shots. By this point the camera anxiety has usually dropped and the looser delivery shows. Some of the most natural content often comes in the final 20 minutes of a session.

6

Transfer Footage to Shared Drive

After the session, AirDrop or USB transfer all footage to the shared Google Drive folder provided by the marketing team. Label each file clearly with the hook concept: AdvisorName_RSUhook_take2.mov. Upload the same day while the session is fresh. The marketing team handles everything from this point forward.
